Our Vision

A business's success depends on the interactions between multiple parties. These interactions between management, suppliers, employees, and ultimately customers are typically taken for granted. We see these as open to negotiation. We see these as open for optimization.

Negotiations can be like a risky battle for precious resources or a passive acceptance of the status-quo. We see them as an opportunity to discover untapped possibilities.

What often keeps negotiaitons from fulfilling their potential are ill-defined preferences, a narrow or short-sighted outlook, and an ever-present skepticism of the human participant on the other side. As impartial mediators, we work with all parties to understand and formalize their preferences. We are "for" both sides.

After a typical negotiaiton, one or both parties are left wondering if more could be done. Our vision is to leverage technology so parties can be sure they've got a great deal and focus instead on their strengths and building meaningful relationships.

Negotiations settled with Florissant mean your employees will quit less often, you'll have more repeat customers, and your suppliers will have your interests in mind.

FAQ

  • What's wrong with human negotiators?

    Nothing is wrongbut are they doing as good of a job as possible? We see negotiations as an opportunity to solve a difficult problem to improve the outcome of two parties. In the context of solving this problem, we see 3 major limits to human negotiators: computational, cognitive bias and emotional.

  • Emotionally Driven?

    There is no getting around our emotional nature, but in this context it can be a disadvantage. How many times have we made errors of judgment because we were angry, jealous or euphoric. These are great feelings, but do we want them impacting our business and limiting the amount of value that we can create?

  • Cognitively Biased?

    In his seminal book Thinking Fast and Slow, Daniel Kahneman, eloquently describes numerous scenarios where we use heuristics, shortcuts, to make decisions and answer difficult questions. One of the most well known, anchoring, is our predisposition to rely on the first piece of information when answering a question. So if we tell you that there are 1500 pages in War and Peace and ask you to make an offer on our product, you become anchored to 1500 and will suggest a number closer to it than otherwise. Imagine how many decisions a human negotiator has to make while keeping track of random, and frequently irrelevant, numbers in his head.

  • Computationally Challanged?

    Numerous advances in artificial intelligence and computational power have made computers better than man at numerous tasks. Thanks to IBM computers can now beat humans at complicated games such as chess and Jeopardy. Nowadays we trust them to execute complicated tasks because they are faster and are able to consider more pieces of information. Consider that programs exist for speech and image recognition or machine learning algorithms that alert customers to unauthorized credit card transactions. In scenarios where a lot of data is available, and many computations need to be made, computers, are better.
    At the end of the day, our human desires drive our economic endeavors. Florissant Advisors, too, is a human company that also suffers from the flaws, we just want to do better.

  • What do you mean by the "valuation" problem?

    Calculating the dollar value of a deal or an exchange. If we agree on a deal where we sell you a car for $10000 above what it cost us, that deal is worth $10000 to us. But what if we're trying to structure a multiyear deal, with numerous delivery dates of various products and quantities? Each of these becomes a parameter in the valuation problem. We can assign a value to each parameter; an agreed upon term in our negotiation. For example, delivery at the end of the month of 1000 red widget. The problem is figuring out which of the possibly many assignments is best for both parties in an exchange. The possible number of assignments is the multiplication across all of your parameters and depends on how many values each of those parameters can take.

    Consider a scenario where we are negotiation over four parameters such as price, quantity, delivery dates, and quality. Each of these parameters has a different number of possible assignment. In the scenario, if we are considering all prices between $1000 and $2000 in $50 increments (20 possible values), any quantity between 6000 and 14000 in 100 unit increments (80 possible values), delivery date on any date in September (30 days) and 5 possible quality ratings that give us a total of (20 * 80 * 30 * 5) 240000 possible assignments.

  • How do traditional negotiation strategies reduce the valuation problem?

    When a human negotiator calculates his BATNA or reserve value, she is explicitly setting a bound on a parameter within a negotiation. Chances are pretty good that the desired negotiated settlement still lies in this bounded solution space, but there is still a small chance, that given the right circumstances, you might want to trade outside of this bounded area.

    Another example is the order with which we resolve issues. For example, in a car buying scenario we might consider features, then price and then financing. That order might means that once we've agreed on a feature it is no longer an issue to be resolved. On the other hand, the resulting agreement could be different if we considered the parameters in a different order, such as financing, price and then features. Which of the two possible outcomes is the better outcome for both parties?
